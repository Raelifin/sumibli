The Sumibli Alphabet

Sumibli is written using 20 letters that each have a "conceptual atmosphere". The letter "g", for instance, is associated with the concepts of action, motion, fire, and the number 5 (among other things). When letters are combined into a word, the meaning and usage of that word can be guessed by the letters that compose it. In this way, the alphabet for Sumibli forms a kind of loose taxonomy of concepts, and it's very helpful to start with the basics of this taxonomy.

By the way, "g" isn't really a letter in the Sumibli alphabet. Rather, 5-g is a letter, and since it sounds like the first sound in "go" we represent it in the latin alphabet as "g". To help remember the latin alphabet versions of the Sumibli letters, we provide an English word that is associated with the letter's conceptual atmosphere and that features the same sound as the letter.

Alphabet Patterns

Twenty letters is a lot to take in, especially when each comes attached to a meaning. It helps if we break up the alphabet into groups of letters based on some simple patterns.

The first six letters form a group of plosives that all have a similar sound. Try sounding out "pa ba ta da ka ga" to yourself. Each plosive involves briefly stopping the air coming out of your lungs. They occur in order, starting from the lips and moving towards the back of the mouth, with every other sound (b, d, g) being "voiced" by action in the throat. (This pattern of voiced consonants occuring right after their unvoiced counterparts ("tada") is a general pattern across the alphabet.)

The number six is important in Sumibli, and there are six plosives. These are the letters most commonly associated with a number, and their symbols in the Sumibli script are drawn with a number of strokes equal to their order in the alphabet.

Now let's consider the next six consonants: n, m, r, l, s, and z. Together with the plosives, these form the standard consonants, which are all drawn with characters that are about three times as wide as they are tall. The standard consonants appear in order of increasing abstraction, and we can tell a story with them of a person who grows up, exploring more complex and distant subjects as they age:

  • p - the new person can percieve and ponder
  • b - and soon they know the bounds of their body and being
  • t - where this and that are at in space and time
  • d - and how devices and dirt are distinct
  • k - in the world they meet creatures like kangaroos and koalas
  • g - they play games of going places and getting goals
  • n - but encounter negative people who do not like the games
  • m - and so myths are manufactured to make
  • r - relational harmony emerge through trading resources
  • l - and allowing feelings of lasting love and belonging
  • s - to give space for studying the secrets of symbols
  • z - like zero and zed and the abstract zen of endings

The final five consonants in the alphabet (f, v, sh, th, and h) are called structural constants because their meanings are mostly grammatical. They provide the words that give sentences their structure and make things comprehensible, without providing content in themselves. Each will be introduced later on. These letters can be larger than the standard consonants and behave differently in Sumibli script.

Lastly, we have the three vowels: i, a, and u. Sumibli doesn't put a lot of emphasis on vowel sounds, and they don't have much meaning. But the general story is that they're lined up from the tightest ("i") to the loosest ("u") and can similarly be seen as going from near to far, with "a" being in the middle.

Pronunciation Tips for Consonants

Sumibli consonants mostly sound as you'd expect from seeing the latin equivalent. But we should briefly mention some things about the last three consonants: sh, th, and h.

The letter "sh" (aka "j") is usually pronounced like the first sound in "shoe" but can also be pronounced like the first sound in "genre" or the last sound in "mirage". In other words, Sumibli doesn't distinguish the voiced and unvoiced sounds.

The letter "th" (aka "x") also can be either voiced or unvoiced, sounding either like "this" or like "with". The voiced version is correct at the start of words, while the unvoiced is correct at the end of words, exactly like in the example of "this" vs "with".

The letter "h" can be silent as long as it's clear which words are which, like in the word "hour" or when a person with a French accent says the word "happy".

Sumibli Atoms

Sumibli words are very small - so small, in fact, that we call them "atoms". This is to suggest a contrast with compound-words that are formed from combining atoms together. A Sumibli atom always starts with a consonant and has exactly one vowel or one diphthong. When we write an atom in Sumibli script, we stack the letters on top of one another to form a glyph. (For the structural consonants other than "h" we sandwich the vowel inside the consonant.)

PA-papa
("pah")		to think
KIk-kikkik
("keek")		a fish
P8a-prapra
("prah")		a person
NUAg-nuagnuag
("nwag")		to sleep
VI-vivi
("vi")		{verb}

Atoms that start with a structural consonant (f, v, sh, th, or h) are structural atoms. Those that start with a standard consonant (p, b, t, d, k, g, n, m, r, l, s, or z) are standard atoms.

üéÆ üîù

Simple Sentences

All standard atoms can be sentences by themselves. For instance, the sentence "kik" means "there is a fish" or "there are fish". Sumibli doesn't force the speaker to give a quantity, so it's vague by default. Tense is also not required, so "kik" can also mean "there were fish" or "there will be fish".

When a noun-like word like "kik" is used like this we think of it being a statement about existence ("a fish exists"). Verb-like words like "nuag" (sleep) can also be thought of as being about existence sometimes. The sentence "nuag" can be translated "someone is sleeping", "there is sleeping", or "a sleeper exists".

But what if we want to say "a fish was sleeping"? For this we use the stucture atom "vi" to mark the verb of the sentence.

KIk-VI-NUAg- kik vi nuaga fish was sleeping
üéÆ üîù

Sentence Order

Sumibli sentences are very flexible. By default the subject comes first, followed by "vi" and then the verb. (When there's no verb, the sentence is about existence/being.) But we can also have the verb come first, followed by "via" and then the subject.

P8a-VI-PA- pravipa pra vi pathe person is thinking
PA-VIA-P8a- pa via prathe person is thinking

We can rearrange the parts of the sentence to emphasize different things, or simply to be poetic. A part of a sentence preceded by an atom that starts with "v" is a phrase (noun phrase, verb phrase, etc). We can even explicitly mark the first phrase in the sentence, even though we usually don't.

VIA-P8a-VI-PA- viapravipa via pra vi pathe person is thinking

Adjectives and Adverbs

Here are a few more words to work with:

("bood")		heavy

Just like with any standard atom, these can be used as verbs.

KIk-VI-BUd- kik vi budthe fish is heavy

And likewise, any standard atom can be used as a noun, including these.

TAm-tamtamthere is a northerner
TAm-VI-NUAg- tam vi nuagthe northerner is asleep
TAm-VI-P8a- tam vi prathe northern one is a person

To use words as adjectives - to modifying some noun without needing a full sentence to modify it - we use a new structure atom: "fu". We put fu in a phrase between the noun and the adjective. And in fact, any standard atom can be used as an adjective, not just the ones that are adjective-like!

KIk-FU-BUd- kik fu budthere was a heavy fish
KIk-FU-LAl-VI-TAm- kik fu lal vi tamthe good fish
are to the north
P8a-FU-PA-VI-LAl- pra fu pa vi lala thinking person

The word "fu" can also be used to modify actions (as adverb) just as easily as it can modify things (as an adjective).

P8a-VI-NUAg-FU-LAl- pra vi nuag fu lalthe person
sleeps correctly

We usually think of "fu" as making one of the words an adjective/adverb, but we can also think of it as talking about the intersection of two concepts. A heavy fish is the insersection of fish and heavy things. To sleep correctly is the intersection of being asleep and being correct.

Compounds

So far we've been considering phrases with a single atom, or ones broken up by "fu" into sub-phrases with one atom each. But now let's consider what happens when two standard atoms come in contact with each other. This structure is called a "compound" and is similar to compound words in English like "snow-man" or "air-plane".

A snowman is not really a man made of snow, and it's certainly not a snowy man. Instead, compounds are new concepts that can have a distinct meaning in the dictionary. For example, we use "kik fu bud" to say "dense fish", but this is a very different thing from "kik bud", which we often just write as "kikbud" when using Latin text. A kikbud is a benthic organism - a creature that lives in the water but mostly sinks rather than swims, like a lobster or a starfish. Kikbud are fish in the sense that they are creatures that live in water (see the philosophy of animals for more), and they're dense in the sense that they sink rather than float, but they're a more specific thing than just dense fish.

KIb- kiborgan
NIk- nikto consume/eat/drink
KIb-NIk- kibnikmouth
KIb-FU-NIk- kib fu nikconsuming organ
(mouth, stomach, gut, etc)

Order matters when forming compounds. A snow-man is not the same as man-snow, and it's the same in Sumibli. We treat the word on the left as the root and the word on the right as the augment. The compound is always a variation on the root, hinted at (sometimes metaphorically) by the augment.

NIk-KIb- nikkibto consume/eat/drink
using a body part

Despite the danger of misusing a compound, Sumibli speakers are encouraged to default to using compounds instead of modifying with "fu". What is a "kikpra"? It's not in the dictionary, but we can guess it means something like "sentient fish". If I want to talk about fish people, "kikpra" serves my goal at least as well as "kik fu pra".

Direct Objects

A couple sections back we introduced the word "nik" which means roughly "to eat", so we can say "kik vi nik" for "the fish eats". But sentences don't just need subjects to do the action, they also sometimes need objects to be acted on. We use the word "va" to mark the direct object in a sentence, just like we use "via" to mark the subject or "vi" for the verb.

KIk-VI-NIk-VA-P8a kik vi nik va praa fish ate the person
P8a-VI-NIk-VA-KIk- pra vi nik va kikpeople eat fish

If "va" is used in a sentence without an explicitly marked verb, we interpret the first part of the sentence as a verb.

NIk-VA-KIk- nik va kikfish are eaten

All Sumibli verbs (and thus all standard atoms and compounds) have room for a direct object. In the case of "nik" it's pretty clear that the direct object is what's being eaten, but can you guess what the direct object of "pra" (person) is?

P8a-VI-P8a-VA-KIk pra vi pra va kikthe person
has the personality
of a fish

There are patterns that help us guess what the relationship is between the subject and the object, but in many cases you'll just need to look up the word in the dictionary to be sure.

Actions as Nouns

It's often useful to talk about relations between actions. For example, "the eater is good" is different from "eating is good". To do this we can use a new structure atom: "vai". The atom "vai" only appears at the start of a sentence or immediately after either "via" or "va". When it appears at the start of the sentence we pretend that there's a "via" before it. In other words, we see "vai" as a modifier to the subject or object of a sentence. Instead of a normal noun phrase, something modified by "vai" becomes a subclause that works much like a full sentence in itself. These subclauses can even be nested!

LAl-VIA-NIk- lal via nikthe eater is good
LAl-VIA-VAI-NIk- lal viavai nikit's good for
there to be an eater
LAl-VIA-VAI-P8a-VI-NIk- lal viavai pra vi nikit's good for
a person to eat
MI-VA-VAI-P8a-VI-NIk- mi vavai pra vi nikthere's talk about
a person eating
MI-VA-VAI-
LAl-VIA-VAI-P8a-VI-NIk-		 mi vavai
lal viavai pra vi nik		there's talk about
it being good for
a person to eat

All these examples of sub-clauses are given at the end of the parent clause because we can't say anything more about the parent once we're inside the subclause. If we want to get around this restriction and escape back to the outer-scope, we can use the atom "viu".

VIA-VAI-P8a-VI-NIk-VIU-
VI-LAl-		 viavai pra vi nik viu

Prepositions

When we want to give additional context to a sentence, we can use subclauses as introduced in the previous section. For instance if we want to say "a fish thinks inside" we can say "(a fish thinks) is inside". But sometimes, especially when quickly composing a sentence during conversation, it's useful to be able to say "a fish thinks... inside", where we see the first part of the sentence as the primary content, and the note about being inside as less-important, additional info.

The atom "vu" is the fastest and most common way of additing context info. It can be seen as a preposition marker, or just a shorthand for the structures of the previous section that doesn't require forethought. The basic rule is that "X vu Y Z" = "viavai X viu vi Y va Z", where X is the whole sentence before the "vu", Y is a single atom, and Z is a phrase. This reduces the number of atoms needed to express a preposition by 80%! Additionally, "vu" can't be nested; if you see another "vu" after the first it's assumed to be more additional info about the same action.

Let's look at some examples:

T8i-triinside
KIk-VI-T8i- kik vi tria fish is inside
KIk-VI-PA-VU-T8i- kik vi pa vutria fish thinks inside
KIk-VI-PA-VU-T8i-NIk- kik vi pa vutri nika fish thinks
inside the eater

Because of the special role of the first atom after the "vu", when writing in latin script we customarily leave out the space.

Indirect Objects

Sometimes we want to not just talk about the direct object of an action, but also indirect objects that are related to some action. For instance, in English we can say "he gave her a toy" and we understand "a toy" as the thing that was given.

Sumibli has no indirect objects, instead relying entirely on prepositions/subclauses. Just as we can say "he gave a toy to her" to communicate the same idea in English, we can use prepositions whenever additional context is needed in Sumibli.

Possession

We've already talked about using words as adjectives using the atom "fu". The way "fu" works is essentially to find the overlap of two sets of things. For example, "kik fu bud" is talking about the intersection of the set of fishes and the set of heavy things.

But sometimes we want to talk about something that is associated with something else. We don't want "the thing that is a mouth and is also a person", but rather "the person's mouth". To do this, we use the word "fa".

BUd-FA-KIk- bud fa kikthe fish's weight
KIk-FA-BUd- kik fa budthe weight's fish
KIb-NIk-FA-P8a- kibnik fa prathe person's mouth

Note that this association is vague. It's not necessarily innate, social, legal, or exclusive.

Answering Questions

Standard questions are yes/no by default. To answer such a question you can say "za", which means "true", or "zan", which means "false". You can also always just make a statement which directly answers the asker's confusion. The word "na" also works as a response that means "the opposite is true".

JAI-KIk-VI-NIk- shai kik vi nikDo fish eat?
KIk- kikThey eat.
JAI-TAm-VI-LAl-
JAI-P8a-LAl-VI-TAm 		shai tam vi lal
shai pralal vi tam		Is the north good?
Are there heroes in the north?
ZAn-JI-NA- zan shi naNo.
There are villains there.

Question askers can also specify specific things they're curious about, using the atom "hau". For more details on this, see the last section on "Attitudinals". When answering such questions, it's conventional to offer responses to in the form of positive statements.

JAI-HAU-VI-TAm shai hau vi tamWhat's to the north?
P8a-LAl- pralalHeroes.

Foreign Words

Not every concept will easily fit into Sumibli. Some words will need to be borrowed from other languages and cultures. This can be done easily with the letter "th". Whenever we want to say a foreign word, we add "th" as a suffix to whatever we were just saying. For example, we can use a loanword as the verb in a sentence by attaching "th" after "vi".

It's up to the writer whether foreign words are written in their native script, or translated into Sumibli script. Regardless, the word should be pronounced according to how the natives say it, even if those sounds aren't supported by the Sumibli alphabet.

KIk-VI- √æ swim kik vith swimfish swim
KIk-VI- √æ SUIm- kik vith swimfish swim

If you want to use foreign text for the subject of a sentence, the "th" suffix can be attached either to an explicit "via" or to an explicit marker for the start of the sentence like "shi".

Because foreign text can have arbitrary sounds, there is no explicit marker for the end of foreign speech; it's up to the speaker to make it clear that they're speaking Sumibli again. When writing, it's customary to include spaces around foreign words to distinguish them.

VIA-√æ sushi VI-LAl- viath sushi vi lalsushi is correct
JI-√æ sushi VI-KIk- shith sushi vi kiksushi is fish

Remember that "th" when used in this way is always pronounced unvoiced, like in "with". When a "th" suffix is added to the end of a word with a trailing consonant, it's permitted to inject a short "uh" sound before the "th" to aid in pronunciation. This added sound is not a letter, and doesn't show up in written Sumibli.

Names

Foreign names work exactly the same way that other foreign words do.

JAI-√æ Max VI-P8a-LAl- shaith Max vi pralalIs Max a hero?

Things can also be given Sumibli names. A Sumibli name is just an atom or compound that's preceded by the atom "thi". Remember to pronounce it with a voiced "th" like the English word "the". When talking about something with a Sumibli name in another language, the "thi" is dropped.

√ûI-SU-MI-B9i-VI-LAl- thi sumibli vi lalSumibli is good

Nicknames are common in Sumibli. In other words, it's fine to use "thi" liberally whenever things are clear. In many cases, this means it is right to use "thi" as the definite article, like "the" in English.

Other Pronouns

Pronouns in Sumibli are based on compressing a name (or long compound) down to one syllable. The atom "tha" marks a pronoun, and the atom following it is the compressed version of the person/thing's name. For instance, if we're talking about "Mitch Halley", instead of giving his full name each time, we could reference him as "tha mil". We start by representing "Mitch Halley" as phonetically written Sumibli: "mitsh hali". (See the full spec for details on transcribing foreign sounds.) We take the first standard consonant and follow it by the first vowel/diphthong. If the first consonant is standard and there's more than one consonant in the name, we also take the final standard consonant.

Note that "Mitch Halley" gets shortened to "mil" but "Mitch" gets shortened to "mit" (because "sh" is structural). Sumibli's pronoun system doesn't specify whether to compress a person's full name just their first/last name.

√ûA-MIl-VI-MIt-VA-√ûA-JAI- tha mil vi mit
va tha shai		"Mil" will talk
to "Shai"

Note that Sumibli pronouns always put the first consonant in front of the first vowel, even when people's names start with a vowel. This including glides/semivowels like "Y" and "W".

Alice is referenced with "tha las".

Wendy Petrov is referenced with "tha nuar" (because "v" is not a structural).

Attitudinals

Sumibli lets us talk about the emotions of people (including the speaker) by using verbs/adjectives/etc. But there's also a shorthand that gives speakers the power to annotate their speech with emotional interjections. These interjections are a bit like emoji/emoticons, and are called "attitudinals". Attitudinals all start with the letter "h" and are attached to the last thing that was mentioned. Unlike standard words, attitudinals aren't making claims about the world, but are purely an expression of emotion, like "yay!" or "boo!".
